The established approach to phonics doesn't work for 1 in 5

Schools use a method called Systematic Synthetic Phonics (SSP), which requires kids to learn the sounds of individual letters.

Government data shows that if your child struggles with that approach, they aren’t alone.

  • 1 in 5 don’t meet the expected phonics standard by age six.
  • And around 1 in 4 don’t meet the expected reading standard at age eleven.

The ‘more of the same’ myth

The parents of struggling kids are told that ‘more of the same’ SSP will work, but most of those children never catch up.

  • Only a quarter of pupils who fail phonics in Year 2 manage to meet the expected reading standard by the end of primary.

The limitations of SSP

Too many children make the difficult transition to secondary school without four foundations of reading:

  1. Phonemic awareness

    Children need to be able to isolate, blend and manipulate sounds in spoken words. It's essential – without it, reading will always elude them.

    To develop this skill, some children need more support than schools can give.

  2. Orthographic knowledge

    To become fluent readers, kids need to understand how to bond speech sounds, spelling, and meaning.

    This isn’t taught. Some kids work it out intuitively. Those that can’t are stuck.

  3. The ability to self-teach

    In the English language there are over four hundred letter-to-sound mappings. Kids are taught the first hundred and are expected to learn the rest themselves.

    Some can intuitively self-teach, while others need support to develop that skill.

  4. Motivation

    ‘Decodable’ books can make reading robotic and restrict vocabulary. If they struggle, kids feel locked in a failure loop.

    It kills their interest and confidence.

Thankfully, there’s a more effective alternative to SSP.

Word mapping: an evolved approach

I’ve seen struggling young readers make better progress by starting with the sounds we use to make words, rather than the sound of letters in isolation.

This innovative approach introduces kids to speech sounds using fun characters, which they love.

It helps them build vital skills that they don't get from SSP, including phonemic awareness and orthographic knowledge, plus the ability to self-teach.

As they explore words of interest, kids discover the joy of reading. And they build self belief to take into the classroom.

In school, and at home

Other educators are seeing how starting with speech sounds can benefit struggling pupils. During term time, I use this approach to support children within primary schools, working one-to-one.

And with training and a little practise, parents can use this approach to support their children outside of school, or if educating at home.

Origins

I use a word mapping framework called Word Mapping Mastery, which was pioneered by Emma Hartnell-Baker. It's an evolution that's based on updated science, rather than an entirely new system.
